# John Templeton — Maximum Pessimism; Buy When No One Else Will; Go Global

> *"The time of maximum pessimism is the best time to buy, and the time of maximum optimism is the best time to sell."* — Sir John Templeton, widely quoted; attributed to him across his interviews and in *Investing the Templeton Way* (Lauren C. Templeton and Scott Phillips, 2008), his authorized primer.

## When this framework applies

Templeton is the first framework Veda reaches for on **`crisis` questions**, on **`buy` questions where a market, sector, or country is in documented distress**, and as the contrarian counter-framework when the user's `self_identified_weakness` is *"panic sells"* or *"can't buy into fear"*. He is the point-of-maximum-pessimism specialist — the discipline that buys Japanese small-caps in 1954, bombed-out European equities after WWII, South Korea in 1998, and US financials selectively in March 2009, each against the consensus that those opportunities were uninvestable. He also supplies the **global mandate** discipline: the refusal to accept home-country bias as a valid portfolio construction. Templeton is **not** the first pick for single-name business-quality framing (Buffett, Fisher, Lynch), intrinsic-value math (Buffett, Klarman), sizing math (Thorp), macro regime reads (Druckenmiller, Dalio), or tail-risk structuring (Taleb). His framework is for **contrarian buying into maximum pessimism with selectivity, long-horizon patience, and a global opportunity set.**

## Core principles

### 1. Buy at the point of maximum pessimism

The canonical rule, stated and restated across sixty years of Templeton interviews. *"People are always asking me where the outlook is good, but that's the wrong question. The right question is: Where is the outlook most miserable?"* (Templeton, attributed in *Investing the Templeton Way*.) The operational logic: at the point of maximum pessimism, forced sellers dominate price-setting and the asset class or country trades below *any* reasonable intrinsic-value estimate — not because the businesses have failed, but because the marginal holder is compelled to sell. The investor with capital and discipline at that moment is buying from forced sellers, which is where outsized long-horizon returns are made. The rule is not *"buy falling knives"* — it is *"buy when valuation has decoupled from fundamentals because of forced selling, and hold through the normalization"*.

### 2. Maximum pessimism must be *identifiable* — not a feel

Templeton's documented examples share three observable markers, which Veda should demand before applying the framework:

- **Valuation at cycle extreme.** Aggregate P/E, P/B, or dividend yield at a multi-decade percentile extreme against its own history. Cite Tier 1–2 data — country-level MSCI indices, Shiller CAPE where applicable, aggregate yield vs. its 10- and 20-year history.
- **Sentiment at extreme bearishness.** Fund flows net outflow, surveys of investor sentiment at decile-low readings, sell-side capitulation pieces published, prominent investors publicly giving up on the asset class. The 2008 "RIP Good Times" Sequoia memo is the canonical sentiment marker for VC; equivalent markers exist at the asset-class and country level.
- **Forced selling mechanism active.** Leverage unwinds, margin calls, ETF redemptions, sovereign pressure, regulatory forced liquidation — a specific, named mechanism that explains why prices are where they are *independent of fundamentals*.

If the user cannot point to all three, they are probably observing a normal drawdown and calling it pessimism. Veda's rule: **`buy` under Templeton requires all three markers to be present with Tier 1–2 evidence, or the verdict is `wait` — pessimism is not yet maximum.**

### 3. Selectivity within the pessimism

Templeton did not buy *everything* at the point of maximum pessimism — he bought what would survive. Each of his canonical trades (post-WWII Europe, 1950s Japan, 1998 Korea, 2000 pre-tech-bubble reallocation, 2008–2009 select US financials) involved company-level selection within the bombed-out market. The operational rule: at the point of maximum pessimism, the screen for *survivable* businesses — clean balance sheets, adequate liquidity, moats intact, management credible — becomes the binding filter, not the P/E. Route Klarman's value-trap screen and Buffett's balance-sheet-and-moat check alongside. This is what separates Templeton from naive contrarianism.

### 4. Global mandate — home-country bias is a cost, not a preference

> *"If you want to have a better performance than the crowd, you must do things differently from the crowd."* — Templeton, widely quoted.

Templeton's 1954 Templeton Growth Fund was one of the earliest explicitly-global mutual funds. His argument: the home-country bias most investors carry is an uncompensated risk — they accept the same risk as a global investor but miss the diversification benefit and the occasional opportunity to buy a country at maximum pessimism while their own market is expensive. The operational rule for Veda: on `portfolio` questions where the user's `markets` field is narrow (e.g., India-only or US-only), surface the home-country-bias cost explicitly — not as a prescription to go global, but as a named cost so the user's decision is informed. For `buy` decisions, the opportunity set under consideration is global; rejecting a globally-cheaper-on-fundamentals name in favor of a locally-familiar-but-expensive name is a Templeton-flagged error.

### 5. Long horizons and patience

Templeton's holding periods ran 4–6 years on average, longer on his highest-conviction positions. The contrarian buy is not a tactical trade — the maximum-pessimism point is identifiable, but the *timing* of the normalization is not. Users who buy at maximum pessimism and expect a 6-month re-rating are running the wrong framework. Templeton's discipline is *years*, not *months*. The corollary: the capital deployed to a Templeton-style buy must not be needed for 3+ years. If it is, the position is speculative regardless of how compelling the valuation.

## Decision rules Veda applies

### On `crisis` questions

1. **Verify the three markers.** Valuation extreme, sentiment extreme, named forced-selling mechanism. If all three are present with Tier 1–2 sourcing, the framework *is* activated. If not, this is a normal drawdown and Templeton's rule does not apply (route Marks for paper-vs-permanent instead).
2. **Selectivity — buy what survives.** At the activated point, apply Klarman's value-trap screen (secular decline, balance-sheet impairment, capital misallocation, governance discount) and Buffett's balance-sheet-and-moat check to the candidate names. The combination of maximum-pessimism pricing *and* Klarman/Buffett-clean businesses is Templeton's hit-rate edge.
3. **Deploy, but not all at once.** Templeton's documented pattern was staged deployment — material initial allocation with reserves for further deterioration. The *"forced seller"* price is often not the absolute low; further leverage unwinds can push 20–30% lower. Staged deployment with a specific add schedule (e.g., *"add 50% at initial entry, 30% if drawdown extends another 20%, 20% reserve for tail"*) is a Templeton-coherent structure.
4. **Horizon commitment.** Tell the user explicitly: this capital is committed for 3+ years. If the user cannot commit the capital on that horizon, the recommendation is not this framework — either reduce size to what they can commit, or route differently.

### On `buy` questions (contrarian single-country / single-sector)

1. **Is this a normal drawdown or documented maximum pessimism?** The three-markers test runs first. Without all three, Templeton defers to the other frameworks.
2. **Survivor selection.** Identify the 3–5 names in the bombed-out market that are most likely to survive and re-rate, via Klarman + Buffett filters. Avoid the index-level buy if possible — at maximum pessimism the index is full of companies that will not survive, and the survivors deliver most of the re-rating. For users without capability for company-level selection, the broad index at genuine maximum pessimism is still defensible but lower-expected-return.
3. **Margin of safety at the extreme.** Since Templeton's edge is price dislocation from fundamentals, the margin of safety at entry should be large — 40%+ against a conservative intrinsic value is the operational minimum. Route `scripts/calc.py margin-of-safety` on the primary candidate.

### On `sell` questions

Templeton is rarely the primary framework on a routine `sell`, but he is routed when:

1. **The mirror rule fires — point of maximum optimism.** *"The time of maximum optimism is the best time to sell."* When the user's holding is in an asset class at a multi-decade valuation extreme *on the upside*, with sentiment and flows at euphoric levels, Templeton's rule is at least a **trim trigger** — not to zero, necessarily, but away from peak concentration. The symmetry with the buy rule is not accidental.
2. **The framework is not applicable on routine winners.** Most winners are not at maximum-optimism extremes; they are compounding at fair-to-rich valuations. Apply Fisher's *"almost never sell"* and Lynch's *"don't pull flowers"* as the default, with Templeton's overlay firing only when all three symmetry markers are present.

### On `portfolio` / `how-much` questions

1. **Home-country bias surface.** If the user is 90%+ in one geography, name the Templeton-implied cost and surface the opportunity cost. Do not prescribe.
2. **Pessimism-to-portfolio allocation rule.** When Templeton's framework is active on a country or sector, the recommended *portfolio* allocation tilts toward that country/sector for new capital — but bounded by Dalio's factor-concentration caps and Thorp's Kelly sizing. Templeton supplies the *direction of tilt*; Thorp and Dalio supply the magnitude.

## What Templeton does NOT cover (explicit boundary)

- **Single-name business quality at normal valuations.** Templeton's edge is at the extremes; for routine `buy` questions with ordinary valuation and ordinary sentiment, route Buffett/Fisher/Lynch. Templeton has nothing to add.
- **Intrinsic-value math.** He requires it as an input (to verify the forced-selling gap), but does not produce it. Route Buffett or Klarman.
- **Cycle identification in the short-term debt cycle sense.** Dalio is more structured. Templeton identifies *extremes* in cycles, not cycles themselves.
- **Position-sizing math.** Thorp.
- **Tactical macro bets over 18–24 months.** Druckenmiller. Templeton's horizon is longer and more patient.
- **Growth-at-any-price theses.** Templeton's discipline was global *value*; paying up for Fisher-grade compounders at full prices is outside his framework.
- **Novice-mode interaction.** Templeton's maximum-pessimism rule is hazardous for novices *unsupervised* — it is the framework that most often sounds like *"buy falling knives"* if the three-markers discipline is not enforced. For novices, Templeton's role should be limited to (a) the **`crisis`** context where forced-selling mechanisms are externally obvious (2008-scale events), and (b) the **home-country-bias** surfacing, which is low-risk and high-value. Weight in novice `framework_weights` is 0.02 per [profile.template.md](../setup/profile.template.md) — *rarely relevant at novice stage* is correct. Surface the discipline in `education_note` when it does fire; do not encourage novices to call "maximum pessimism" on their own read.

## Interaction with other frameworks

- **Paired with Marks** on `crisis` questions. Marks supplies the *paper-vs-permanent* filter; Templeton supplies the *maximum-pessimism contrarian-buy* trigger. Consensus between them — drawdown is paper, not permanent, AND maximum pessimism markers all active — is a high-conviction deployment signal.
- **Paired with Klarman** on contrarian buys. Templeton supplies the direction (*buy this country/sector*); Klarman supplies the selection discipline (*buy only the survivors, clear the value-trap screen*).
- **Paired with Buffett** on *"greedy when fearful"* applications. The two sound alike; Templeton is more explicit about country- and sector-level forced-selling, Buffett is more explicit about business-quality-at-a-fair-price. At a true maximum-pessimism moment, both typically agree.
- **Paired with Druckenmiller** on `hold_check` after a Templeton-style buy. Druckenmiller's *first loss = best loss* applies if the thesis was wrong (balance sheet misread, moat collapsing). Templeton's patience applies if the thesis is intact and the price is still moving against the position. The difference is *thesis-status*, not time-elapsed.

## Worked example — applying Templeton

**Question:** *"Chinese equities are hated. Is this a Templeton moment?"*

**Templeton's process:**

1. **Three-markers test.**
   - Valuation extreme: MSCI China P/E, P/B, dividend yield vs. 10- and 20-year history. If at decile-low readings, marker is active. Tier 1–2: MSCI fact sheets, index provider data.
   - Sentiment extreme: Fund flows from EM-ex-China ETFs, investor surveys, prominent sell-side pieces declaring China uninvestable. Multiple markers over 2022–2024.
   - Forced-selling mechanism: Passive index reweighting out of China (MSCI moves), regulatory-driven redemptions, de-listing threats, geopolitical risk premium forcing institutional divestment. These are real, named, Tier 1 sources.

   All three plausibly active. Templeton's framework *is* activated — but the user must verify with current data.
2. **Survivor selection.** Route Klarman. Chinese equity risk categories: governance discount (real and large for some names; VIE structure questions; state-intervention risk on platforms), balance-sheet (generally clean for the large internet names; varies for property and financials), secular decline (property sector yes, consumer platforms no), capital allocation (varies). The broad index buys the governance risk at scale; the company-level buy in names with cleaner structures and stronger moats is the higher-expected-return trade.
3. **Margin of safety.** Against what conservative intrinsic value? Route Buffett's range-based DCF per name. Large Chinese internet names at 8–12x forward earnings with ~20%+ of market cap in net cash clear a 40%+ MoS on even the conservative case — the Templeton gap is present.
4. **Horizon commitment.** 3+ years. State explicitly that the re-rating timing is unknown and capital should not be needed sooner. The position is a multi-year hold, not a tactical trade.
5. **Staged deployment.** Recommend staged deployment with a defined add schedule against further drawdown.

**Templeton's verdict (sketch):** Activated. **Buy** selected survivor names at moderate initial allocation, staged, 3+ year horizon, with a named kill criterion: *"If the governance risk materializes as a specific confiscation / forced delisting event at the names held, the thesis is broken and first-loss-best-loss applies (route Druckenmiller)."* **Would be wrong if:** the three-markers test is read wrong on current data (user must verify against Tier 1–2 sources this session, not trust the 2022–2024 example), OR the survivor-selection work proves the candidate names have moat erosion or balance-sheet deterioration the thesis did not anticipate.
